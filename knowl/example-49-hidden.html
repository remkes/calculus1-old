Take the quartic polynomial \(f(x) = x^4+8x^3-270x^2+10\text{.}\)

  1. The domain is all of \(\RR\text{.}\)

  2. The range is bounded below, since is it a quartic with a positive leading coefficient. However, that minimum is not obvious at first glance.

  3. The function is continuous.

  4. \((0,1)\) is the \(y\)-intercept. \(f\) has four roots, but calculating them is difficult. A computer approximation shows roots near \(x\) values of \(0.2\text{,}\) \(-0.2\text{,}\) \(-21\) and \(13\text{.}\)

  5. There are no undefined points, so there are no vertical asymptotes. The limit at \(\pm \infty\) is \(\infty\text{,}\) so there are no horizontal asymptotes.

  6. The first derivative is \(f^\prime (x) = 4x^3 + 24x^2 - 540x\text{,}\) which has roots of \(0\text{,}\) \(9\) and \(-15\text{.}\) The critical points are \((0, 1)\text{,}\) \((9, -9476)\) and \((-15, -37124)\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} \amp (-\infty, -15) \amp \amp (-15,0) \amp \amp(0,9) \amp \amp (9, \infty) \\ \amp f^\prime(-16) = -18880 \amp \amp f^\prime(-1) = 560 \amp \amp f^\prime(1) = -512 \amp \amp f^\prime(10) = 1000 \\ \amp f^\prime(x) \lt 0 \amp \amp f^\prime(x) \gt 0 \amp \amp f^\prime(x) \lt 0 \amp \amp f^\prime(x) \gt 0 \\ \amp f(x) \text{ is decreasing} \amp \amp f(x) \text{ is increasing} \amp \amp f(x) \text{is decreasing} \amp \amp f(x) \text{ is increasing} \end{align*}

    \((0,1)\) is the only maximum and the points \((-15, -37124)\) and \((9, -9476)\) are mimima.

  7. The second derivative is \(f^{\prime \prime}(x) = 12x^2 + 48 x - 540\text{,}\) which has roots at \(x\) values \(5\) and \(-9\text{.}\) That gives possible inflection points at \((5, -5124)\) and \((-9, -21140)\text{.}\)

    \begin{align*} \amp (-\infty, -9) \amp \amp (-9,5) \amp \amp (5, \infty)\\ \amp f^{\prime\prime}(-10) = 180 \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime}(0) = -540 \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime}(6) = 180\\ \amp f^{\prime\prime}(x) \gt 0 \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime}(x) \lt 0 \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime} (x) \gt 0 \\ \amp f(x) \text{ is concave up} \amp \amp f(x) \text{ is concave down} \amp \amp f(x) \text{ is concave up} \end{align*}
