We have a possible maximum net benefit when the marginal cost and marginal benefit are equal. We calculate those two and set them equal to each other.

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} C(x) \amp = \frac{1}{2} x \\ \frac{d}{dx} B(x) \amp = \frac{4}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{x}}\\ \frac{1}{2} x \amp = \frac{2}{\sqrt{x}}\\ x^\frac{3}{2} \amp = 4\\ x \amp = 4^\frac{2}{3} = \sqrt[3]{16} \doteq 2.52 \end{align*}

Then we look at intervals. Below this critical value, say at \(x=1\text{,}\) the marginal benefit is 2 and the marginal cost is \(\frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) Here the net benefit is increasing, since the marginal benefit exceeds the marginal cost. Above the critical value, say at \(x=4\text{,}\) the marginal cost is 2 and the marginal benefit is 1. The marginal cost exceeds the marginal benefit, so the net benefit is decreasing. We conclude that we have a maximum net benefit at approximatly \(x = 2.52\)2 and the marginal benefit is 1. The marginal cost exceeds the marginal benefit, so the net benefit is decreasing.
