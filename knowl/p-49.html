If two lines in the plane are not parallel or identitcal, there will be exactly one point where they meet. This is called the intersection point. In FigureÂ 1.4.1, we have the lines \(y=x+1\) and \(y=3-3x\) and their intersection point at \((2,3)\text{.}\) This is the intersection point because it is the only point where the coordinates satisfy both equations. Algebraically, we can find this point by solving the system composed of both equation.

\begin{align*} y \amp = x+1\\ y \amp = 3x-3\\ x+1 \amp = 3x-3\\ -2x \amp = -4\\ x \amp = 2\\ y \amp = x+1 = 2+1 = 3 \end{align*}

The intersection point is \((2,3)\text{.}\)
