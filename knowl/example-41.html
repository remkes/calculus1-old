Example 11.1.2.

Consider the quadratic \(f(x) = x^2 + 2\text{.}\) The derivative is \(f^\prime(x) = 2x\text{.}\) To find the critical points, we set the derivative equal to zero.

\begin{align*} f(x) \amp = x^2 + 2\\ f^\prime(x) \amp = 2x\\ 2x \amp = 0 \implies x = 0 \end{align*}

So \(x=0\) is the only critical point. After we have found a critical point, we need to determine if it is a maximum, minimum, or neither. We do this by looking at the sign of the derivative near the critical point. The easiest way to record this information is in intevals of increase and decrease. We divide the domain of the function into pieces seperated by the critical points.

In this example, the domain is \(\RR\) and the only critical point is \(x=0\text{.}\) Splitting the domain by that critical point gives us the intervals \((-\infty, 0)\) and \((0,\infty)\text{.}\) On these intervals, the derivative will either be positive or negative, so the function will either be increasing or decreasing respectively. For this example, \(f^\prime(x) = 2x\text{,}\) which is negative on \((-\infty,0)\) and positive on \((0, \infty)\text{;}\) therefore, the function is decreasing on \((-\infty,0)\) and increasing on \((0, \infty)\text{.}\)

This lets us classify the critical point. At \(x=0\text{,}\) the function switches from decreasing to increasing. This means that \(x\) must be a minimum. We can conclude that \((0,2)\) is a local minimum of the function.
