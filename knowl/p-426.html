The limit defining the derivative may not always exist. If it does exist at a point \(a\) in the domain, we say that \(f\) is differentiable at the point \(a\text{.}\) If it exists for all points in the domain of \(f\text{,}\) we say that \(f\) is a differentiable function. Differentiability requires continuity: if a function makes a sudden jump, it doesn't make sense to talk about a rate of change and a tangent line cannot be defined. Differentiability also requires a ‘smoothness’ condition. A function whose graph has sharp corners is not differentiable at the sharp corners, because it doesn't make sense to define a tangent line at a sharp corner. The graph of the function must be smooth. Figure 6.2.2 shows how a jump or sharp corner makes the choice of a tangent line problematic.
