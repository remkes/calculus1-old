Many functions are not monotonic, but we would like to invert them anyway. We solve this problem by restricting the domain. The classic example is the quadratic \(f(x) = x^2\text{.}\) This function has a domain of \(\RR\text{,}\) but it is not invertible, since both \(x\) and \(-x\) are sent to \(x^2\text{.}\) Going backwards, we do not know whether to sent \(x^2\) to \(x\) or \(-x\text{.}\) Therefore, we restrict the domain. The function \(f(x) = x^2\) on the restricted domain \([0, \infty)\) is increasing, therefore it is invertible. Its inverse is \(f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}\text{,}\) returning the positive square root.
