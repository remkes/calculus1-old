When \(x > 2 \text{,}\) the logarithm \(\ln (x-1)\) is positive and growing, so its reciprocal is positive and decreasing. The function is invertible on this domain. We follow the steps of the process: set \(y = f(x)\text{,}\) solve for \(y\text{,}\) then switch variables to write the inverse as a function of our conventional independent variables \(x\text{.}\) (In the step from the first to the second line, we simply take the reciprocal of each side of the equation; when the numerators are not zero, this is a valid operation).

\begin{align*} y \amp = \frac{1}{\ln (x-1)}\\ \frac{1}{y} \amp = \ln (x-1)\\ e^{\frac{1}{y}} \amp = x-1 \\ e^{\frac{1}{y}} + 1 \amp = x \\ f^{-1}(x) \amp = e^{\frac{1}{x}} + 1 \end{align*}
