Example 9.1.1.
\begin{equation*} \int 2x (x^2+1)^4 dx \end{equation*}

This integral involves a composition. Label the inside function \(u = x^2 + 1\text{.}\) Then we change the entire integral from the variable \(x\) to the varaibles \(u\text{.}\) This is a substitution, hence the rule is called the substitution rule. We also need to change the differential term \(dx\text{.}\) This term is strange and confusing, and we really don't have the room and energy to go into all the historical subtleties of differentials. If \(u = u(x)\) is the relationthip between \(u\) and \(x\text{,}\) then \(du = u^\prime(x) dx\) is the relationship between \(dx\) and \(du\text{.}\) Here \(u = x^2 +1\text{,}\) so \(du = (2x) dx\text{.}\) Let's rewrite the original integral.

\begin{equation*} \int (x^2+1)^4 (2x)dx \end{equation*}

We can see that substitution works well here: we can replace \(x^2 +1\) with \(u\) and \((2x) dx\) with \(du\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \int u^4 du \end{equation*}

We can find the anti-derivative easily by reversing the power rule.

\begin{equation*} \int u^4 du = \frac{u^5}{5} + c \end{equation*}

Then we undo the substitution, by replacing \(u\) with \(x^2+4\text{.}\)

\begin{equation*} \int 2x (x^2+1)^4 dx = \int u^4 du = \frac{u^5}{5} + c = \frac{(x^2+4)^5}{5} + c \end{equation*}
