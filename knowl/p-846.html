Second Derivative: We differentiate again. For ease of working with is for the future steps, I'll again factor out the exponential term and collect the polynomial terms after taking the derivative.

\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx} (e^{-x} (-x^2 - x + 9)) \amp \\ \amp = \left( \frac{d}{dx} e^{-x} \right) (-x^2 - x + 9) + (e^{-x}) \left( \frac{d}{dx} -x^2 - x + 9 \right) \\ \amp = -e^{-x} (-x^2 - x + 9) + (e^{-x}) (-2x - 1)\\ \amp = e^{-x} (x^2 + x - 9 - 2x - 1) \\ \amp = e^{-x} (x^2 - x - 10) \end{align*}

We set the second derivative equal to zero and solve for the potential inflection points. Notes, again, that the exponential can never be zero, so we only need to look at the quadratic.

\begin{align*} e^{-x} (x^2 - x - 10) \amp = 0 \\ x^2 - x - 10 \amp = 0 \\ x \amp = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 40}}{2} = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{41}}{2} \end{align*}

We have two potential infelction points. Their approximate values ate \(x \doteq -2.702\) and \(x \doteq 3.702\text{.}\) We move on to the intervals. The domain is all reals, so we have three intervals. Note also that the exponential term is always positive, so the sign of the second derivative is entirely determined by the quadratic.

\begin{align*} \amp \left( -\infty, \frac{1 - \sqrt{41}}{2} \right) \amp \amp \left( \frac{1 - \sqrt{41}}{2}, \frac{1 + \sqrt{41}}{2} \right) \amp \amp \left( \frac{1 + \sqrt{41}}{2}, \infty \right)\\ \amp f^{\prime\prime} (-3) = e^3 (9 + 3 - 10) \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime} (0) = e^0 (0-0-10) \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime} (4) = e^{-4} (16 - 4 - 10) \\ \amp f^{\prime\prime} (-4) = e^3 (2) \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime} (0) = -10 \amp \amp f^{\prime\prime} (3) = e^{-4} (2) \\ \amp \text{Positive} \amp \amp \text{Negative} \amp \amp \text{Positive} \\ \amp f \text{ is concave up.} \amp \amp f \text{ is concave down.} \amp \amp f \text{ is concave up.} \end{align*}

This determines the concavity.
