Section 13.1 How to Write a Math Assignment

Subsection 13.1.1 Academing Writing in Mathematics

Writing a math assignment at the university lebel is a very different thing from your previous experience with mathematics assignments. In this section, I want to give some clear expectation on what I expect for academic writing in mathematics.

Most of this sections follows from one basic idea.

An assignment is a clear and professional presentation of your own arguments and ideas about a set of mathematical problems.
In that context, these are my expectations for assignments

  • Your assignments should be clean, legible and well organized. Don't hand in work with crossed-out sections or questions out of order. (This is part of what I mean by professional in the definition above.)
  • Don't expect to hand in the paper that your start your work on. First work out your solutions in rough; then organize your solutions and write up a complete, clear and well-structure good copy to hand in.
  • Annotate your calculations and explain your work. A string of calculations without any explanation is usually not sufficient. In the definition, I said that an assignment is your argument; arguments require words and sentences, not just mathematical symbols.
  • As a starting rule-of-thump, most first and second year math assignments should be roughly 50% calculations and 50% words and sentences.
  • In the writing you do between your calculation, hold yourself to the same standards you would for any academic writing. Use complete sentences and strive for clear, concise and formal writing.
  • For the vast majority of your work, you can leave your answers in exact values. (For example, write \(\sqrt{5}\) instead of the approximate value \(2.236\text{.}\))
  • When you use a computer algebra system or other technical tool for calculations, briefly indicate that you've done so.
  • If you use any external sources, you must reference them. This includes online courses, videos, and online discussion boards. I am not particular on what reference formatting you use: use any of the standard formats. (There will be more on this in ).
  • If you use a resource which introduces definitions or solution techniques that are different from what we use in this course, you need to explain those definitions or techniques and tell me why they apply to the question that I assigned.
  • Collaboration on assignments is strongly encouraged. However, you must write up your own version of the assignment after you have worked together to figure out a solution. Assignemnts from different students which have word-for-word identical sentences are not acceptable. (And recall that I expect assignments to have many sentences).
  • If you collaborated with other students in the course, please record who you worked with somewhere on the assignment.
  • Hand in your assignment when they are due. If a health or emergency situation prevents you from finishing your assignment, let me know (by email, preferably) as early as possible.
  • For assignments submitted online, submit a PDF scan of your assignment. Almost all mobile devices (phone, tablets) have easy ways to use their built-in cameras to scan documents. Try to find scanning methods that give reasonable file sizes.

Subsection 13.1.2 Examples

To help make these expectation clear, I have a number of examples of these expectations. In each of these examples, Solution 1 is technically correct but very poorly presented. I would not give full marks for this solutions. Solution 2 contains the same technical details, but it must more clearly presented and includes references. Solution 2 would be given full marks.

Example 13.1.1.

Solve this system of equations.

Solution 1

Here is a technically correct but very poor answer.

\begin{align*} x + y \amp = 1 \\ x \amp 1 - y \\ 1 - y + z \amp = 0 \\ y \amp = 1 + z \\ 1 + z + z \amp = 2 \\ 2z \amp = 1 \\ z \amp = \frac{1}{2} \\ y \amp = \frac{3}{2}\\ x \amp = \frac{-1}{2} \end{align*}
Solution 2

Here is a annotated solution, which is much better.

First, I'll solve for \(x\) in the first equation.

\begin{equation*} x = 1 - y \end{equation*}

Then I put this expresion for \(x\) into the second equation.

\begin{equation*} 1 - y + z = 0 \end{equation*}

Now I can solve for \(y\) in this second equation.

\begin{equation*} y = 1 + z \end{equation*}

I can put this expression for \(y\) into the third equation.

\begin{equation*} 1 + z + z = 2 \end{equation*}

Now \(z\) is the only variable, so I can simply solve for it.

\begin{equation*} z = \frac{1}{2} \end{equation*}

I can use this value for \(z\) to determine \(y\) using a previous expression.

\begin{equation*} y = 1 + z = \frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}

Finally, I can use this value for \(y\) to determine \(x\) using the first equation.

\begin{equation*} x = 1 - y = \frac{-1}{2} \end{equation*}

That gives me the full solution.

\begin{align*} \amp x = \frac{-1}{2} \amp \amp y = \frac{3}{2} \amp \amp z = \frac{1}{2} \end{align*}
Example 13.1.2.
How many \(15cm\)-wide planks do you need to fence in a sqaure with area \(9m^2\text{.}\)
Solution 1
\begin{align*} 9 \amp = 3^2 \\ 3 \times 4 \amp = 12 \\ 12 \amp \rightarrow 1200\\ \frac{1200}{15} \amp = 80 \end{align*}
Solution 2

I have been given the area, but I need to know the perimter. This is a square, so the area is the square of the side length. Since the area is \(9m^2\text{,}\) the side length must be \(3m\text{,}\) since \(3^3 = 9\text{.}\) We have four sides, each with length \(3m\text{,}\) so the total perimeter is \(12m\text{.}\) The planks are in \(cm\text{,}\) so we need to write \(12m\) as \(1200cm\text{.}\) Assume that the fence is built of planks that touch without any gaps, we can just divide the perimeter by the plank width.

\begin{equation*} \frac{1200}{15} = 80 \end{equation*}

We need 80 planks. It may not be necessary in the solution, but we can notice, also, that this gives 20 planks for each side, divided evenly, so we don't have to worry about using part of a plank for one side and part for another side.
